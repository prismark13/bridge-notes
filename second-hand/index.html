Second-Hand Play

Second hand low, third hand high. You play before your partner, who plays last — so spend a high card only when playing low would cost a specific trick. The nine counts as an honor.

The four rules
  1. Second hand low. Play your smallest on a low card led; partner plays last.
  2. Cover to promote. Cover an honor only when it turns one of your side's small cards (yours or partner's) into a winner.
  3. Cover the last of touching honors. Duck the earlier ones, cover the last.
  4. Split three, never two. From three touching honors play the lowest of your sequence; from only two, play low.

◆Fine print — where experts differ
Split high or lowStyle
You split the lowest of your sequence (♠ Q J 10 → the 10) — the count-consistent, Kantar style. Plenty of experts split the top instead; both stop the finesse. It's a partnership agreement, not a right-or-wrong.
Covering the tenCareful
Covering a ten (or nine) with a king is the most double-edged cover. Do it to promote partner's nine or jack — not when declarer rates to hold A-Q-J, where you'd just feed him.
Play in tempoTell
Decide before the card is led. A pause tells a sharp declarer you hold the card he's worried about.
Rising: more than the basicsAdvanced
Also rise to knock out dummy's long suit before its entries are set, or to give partner a ruff. And the surround play: holding K‑10‑9 over dummy's jack, insert the 10 to trap it — an intermediate can beat dummy's honor.

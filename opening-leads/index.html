Opening Leads

The one card you play blind — before you see dummy. Good leads come from the auction more than a rulebook, but these get you most of the way. Against notrump you attack; against a suit you play safe.

The four rules

  1. Against notrump, lead length. Build your longest, strongest suit — third or fifth best — or partner's.
  2. Against a suit, lead safe and fast. Sequences and partner's suit are your friends; never underlead an ace.
  3. The card tells the story. Top of a sequence, third or fifth best from length — so partner can read your hand.
  4. Read the auction. Lead the unbid suit, avoid the opponents' suits, and go passive against a confident auction.

IWhich suit — against notrump

No trumps to stop you, so build length tricks.

Your longest, strongest suitLead length
You hold ♠ K J 8 4 2 — lead the 2 (fifth-best, a five-card suit), aiming to set up the long spades.
In notrump the plan is length: knock out stoppers and cash small cards once the suit is established.
Partner has bid a suitPartner's suit
Partner overcalled hearts — lead a ♥, usually in preference to your own suit. Lead your normal card in it (top of a sequence, else low).
Partner told you where their strength is; combining suits beats going it alone.
You hold a strong sequenceLead the sequence
♣ Q J 10 8 — lead the queen. A near-solid sequence is safe and drives out stoppers even if another suit is longer.
Sequences give up nothing and attack at the same time.

IIWhich suit — against a suit contract

Trumps loom, so favor safety and quick tricks over slow length.

Top of a sequenceSequence
♥ K Q J 4 — lead the king. Constructive and gives nothing away.
The safest attacking lead against a trump contract.
Partner has bid a suitPartner's suit
Lead partner's suit — especially if it was raised. Your normal card: top of a sequence, high from a doubleton, else low.
A singleton, for a ruffSingleton
You hold a singleton ♦ 3 and the ♠ A of trumps — lead the 3, win the trump ace, and take your ruff.
A singleton only pays if you have an entry (often a trump trick) to get in and ruff. Without one, it just helps declarer.
A trump, to cut ruffsTrump
When the auction points to a crossruff (two fits, a dummy about to ruff), or you have nothing safe elsewhere, lead a trump.
Reducing dummy's ruffing power can be worth more than a side-suit trick. Don't lead a trump from a holding like Qxx that could cost.
Away from an ace or a lone honorAvoid
Never underlead an ace against a suit — from ♦ A 8 5 lead the ace or pick another suit; lead the low card and you may never score it.
Leading away from unsupported honors (K x x, Q x x) into declarer often gives a trick. Prefer a sequence, partner's suit, or a safe exit.

IIIWhich card to lead

Once you've picked the suit, the card is a signal to partner.

Three or more touching honorsTop of the sequence
♠ K Q J 5 → K;   ♥ Q J 10 4 → Q;   ♦ J 10 9 6 → J.
Broken / interior sequence — lead the top of the touching part: ♣ K J 10 3 → J;   ♠ K Q 10 4 → K.
A long suit with an honor, no sequenceThird / fifth best
From three or four cards, lead your third-best: ♠ K J 8 4 → the 8.
From five or more, lead your fifth-best: ♠ K J 8 5 2 → the 2.
You play third-and-fifth-best leads, so partner counts with the Rule of 12 (third-best) or the Rule of 10 (fifth-best) — subtract your card from 12 or 10 for the higher cards elsewhere.
A doubleton, or three smallHigh / third-best
Doubleton ♥ 9 4 → the 9 (high, then the 4 — a high-low showing the doubleton).
Three small ♣ 8 5 2 → the 2 (third-best — the same count as your longer-suit leads).
From A‑KTop honor
Against a suit, lead from ♠ A K 6 4 — the ace (asking attitude) or the king (asking count): agree which with partner. From a doubleton ♠ A K, lead the king then the ace.
Against notrump you usually lead third or fifth best from A‑K unless the suit is a solid sequence.

IVReading the auction

The bidding is the biggest clue you get — use it.

Choose an unbid suitUnbid suit
After 1♠ – 2♦ – 2♠ – 4♠, hearts and clubs are unbid — prefer one of those over declarer's spades or dummy's diamonds.
Leading the opponents' suits usually helps them; a fresh suit is safer and may find partner's values.
A confident auction, or a slamPassive vs active
When the opponents bid a slam or sound sure of themselves, lead safe — a trump or a sequence — and don't hand over a trick. When you have strength and time, attack your long suit instead.
Match the risk of your lead to the auction: give nothing away when they're strong; build tricks when the hand is a fight.

The hard one. No rule wins every time — the auction, the vulnerability, and your own hand all pull at the choice. Treat these as the default and let the bidding talk you out of them. Back to Bridge Notes.

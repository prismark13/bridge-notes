Bridge · Defensive Card Play

Second-Hand Play

What to do when you are second of the four players to a trick — and your partner still has the last word.

“Second hand low, third hand high.”

You play before your partner, who sits last and sees the whole trick. So spend a high card only when playing low would cost you a specific trick. Everything below is either that default or a reason to break it. Throughout, the nine counts as an honor.

I.A low card is led

Let declarer commit first; make the guessing his problem, not yours.

Play low by default. Dummy ♠ J 7 6, you ♠ K 10 4 — on the 6, play the 4, and let declarer guess where your king sits. Winning a trick you don't have to only clears the position up for him.

Keep your ace. Dummy ♥ K 8 6, you ♥ A 10 7 — on declarer's 2, play low. An ace is for capturing an honor, not a spot card; grabbing it just promotes dummy's king. Taking the ace second-hand is wrong about nine times in ten.

Split three, never two. From ♠ Q J 10 7 play the queen; from ♠ K 10 9 7 the ten — the top of your touching cards. But from only two honors, ♠ Q J 8 3, play low. Splitting three blocks a cheap finesse of your middle cards; splitting two just hands declarer a tempo.

Rise with a reason. Go up in second seat only to cash a winner about to be ruffed or discarded, to deny declarer a trick he couldn't otherwise take, or to knock out dummy's last entry to a long suit.

II.An honor is led toward you

Now the question is whether to cover. Cover only when doing so turns one of your side's smaller cards into a winner — otherwise hold your honor and keep declarer in the dark.

Cover when —

You hold two honors above it. Dummy ♠ J 10 8 6, you ♠ A Q 2 — on the jack, play the ace. You capture a card that would otherwise score; your queen was dominated by declarer's king anyway.

The honor stands alone. An unsupported honor has no touching card beside it, so covering promotes your side's smaller cards. Dummy ♠ 10 5 4, you ♠ K 7 2 — cover the ten. Or dummy ♠ Q 4 2, you ♠ K 9 5 — cover the queen, forcing the ace and promoting partner's ten with your nine.

Don't cover when —

The honor is supported. Dummy ♠ J 10 8, you ♠ K 2 — the ten sits beside the jack, so covering only makes that ten good. Play low.

Dummy holds a solid run. Dummy ♠ Q J 10 9, you ♠ K 8 4 — cover and you turn J-10-9 into three winners for declarer. Duck, and your king takes the third round.

Your honor can't promote. A small doubleton under strength — dummy ♦ A K 10 9, you ♦ J 4 — gives up the jack for nothing; play the 4. So does covering when partner is marked short (a singleton or void as declarer draws trumps), or from length opposite a short dummy, dummy ♠ Q 6, you ♠ K 9 5 4 — where covering only builds declarer's long suit and can crash partner's honor.

Cover the last of touching honors

When the hand leading has two honors in a row, duck the first and cover the last. Dummy ♣ Q J 9, you ♣ K 6 5 — pass the queen, then cover the jack. This is the defense to a double finesse: dummy ♣ Q J 3, you ♣ K 6 2, partner ♣ 10 9 7 5 — let the queen run, cover the jack, and partner's ten is now high. Cover the first honor instead and dummy's jack simply finesses that ten away.

When declarer leads it, read the dummy

Cover if dummy shows a card touching the lead — dummy ♠ A J 10 6, you ♠ K 5 2; on the 9, cover, since dummy's ten backs it up. Play low if dummy has no touching card — dummy ♠ A 8 6 4, you ♠ K 7 2; on the 10, duck, because declarer holds the jack and covering would help only him.

♠ ♦ ♥ ♣

Advanced — the surround play. With an interior holding like K‑10‑9 sitting over dummy's jack, playing an intermediate — the ten — rather than low or the king can trap dummy's honor. Situational; count the suit before reaching for it.

Bridge · Defensive Card Play

Second-Hand Play

Rules to drill. Read the rule, decide your play, then open it for the card layout — the nine counts as an honor throughout.

“Second hand low, third hand high.”

You play before your partner, who sits last and sees the whole trick. Spend a high card only when playing low would cost a specific trick.

I.A low card is led

Let declarer commit first; make the guessing his problem.

No reason to rise → play low
Dummy ♠ J 7 6 · you ♠ K 10 4 — on the 6, play the 4.
Make declarer guess where your king sits; winning now only clears the position up for him.
You hold an ace over the lead → duck
Dummy ♥ K 8 6 · you ♥ A 10 7 — on declarer's 2, play low.
An ace captures an honor, not a spot card. Grabbing it just promotes dummy's king — wrong about nine times in ten.
Three touching honors, not two → split exception
From ♠ Q J 10 7 play the queen; from ♠ K 10 9 7 the ten — the top of your touching cards.
But from only two honors, ♠ Q J 8 3, play low.
Splitting three blocks a cheap finesse of your middle cards; splitting two just hands declarer a tempo.
A concrete reason to win → rise exception
Go up in second seat only to: cash a winner about to be ruffed or discarded; deny declarer a trick he couldn't otherwise take; or knock out dummy's last entry to a long suit.

II.An honor is led toward you

Cover only when it turns one of your side's smaller cards into a winner.

Cover when —

Two honors above the card led → cover, highest
Dummy ♠ J 10 8 6 · you ♠ A Q 2 — on the jack, play the ace.
You capture a card that would otherwise score; your queen was dominated by declarer's king anyway.
A lone, unsupported honor → cover
Dummy ♠ 10 5 4 · you ♠ K 7 2 — cover the ten.
Dummy ♠ Q 4 2 · you ♠ K 9 5 — cover the queen, forcing the ace and promoting partner's ten with your nine.
An unsupported honor has no touching card beside it, so covering promotes your side's smaller cards.

Don't cover when —

The honor is supported → don't cover
Dummy ♠ J 10 8 · you ♠ K 2 — the ten sits beside the jack, so covering only makes that ten good. Play low.
Dummy holds a solid run → don't cover
Dummy ♠ Q J 10 9 · you ♠ K 8 4 — cover and you turn J-10-9 into three winners for declarer. Duck, and your king takes the third round.
Nothing of yours can promote → don't cover
Small doubleton under strength: dummy ♦ A K 10 9 · you ♦ J 4 — the jack promotes nothing; play the 4.
Length opposite a short dummy: dummy ♠ Q 6 · you ♠ K 9 5 4 — covering only builds declarer's long suit and can crash partner's honor.
Same when partner is marked short (a singleton or void as declarer draws trumps): there's no card of partner's to promote.

Timing & who leads it

Touching honors led at you → cover the last double finesse
Dummy ♣ Q J 9 · you ♣ K 6 5 — pass the queen, then cover the jack.
The double finesse: dummy ♣ Q J 3 · you ♣ K 6 2 · partner ♣ 10 9 7 5 — let the queen run, cover the jack, and partner's ten is now high.
Cover the first honor instead and dummy's jack simply finesses that ten away.
Declarer (not dummy) leads it → read the dummy
Touching card in dummy — cover: dummy ♠ A J 10 6 · you ♠ K 5 2 — on the 9, cover, since dummy's ten backs it up.
No touching card — duck: dummy ♠ A 8 6 4 · you ♠ K 7 2 — on the 10, play low; declarer holds the jack, so covering helps only him.

♠ ♦ ♥ ♣

Advanced — the surround play. With an interior holding like K‑10‑9 over dummy's jack, playing an intermediate — the ten — rather than low or the king can trap dummy's honor. Situational; count the suit first.

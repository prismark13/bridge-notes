Bridge · defensive card play

Second-Hand Play & Covering Honors

  1. Second hand low. On a low card led, play your smallest — partner plays last and can capture whatever declarer wastes.
  2. Cover to promote. Cover an honor only when it makes one of your side's smaller cards a winner. Otherwise, hold it.
  3. Cover the last of touching honors. If the leader has two in a row, duck the first and cover the second.
  4. Split three, never two. With three honors (two touching) play the top one; with only two, play low. Never grab an ace on a spot card.
Green — hold back: play low, duck, or don't cover Orange — act: cover, split, or rise

Three+ honors, two touching Exception
Split
You♠ Q J 10 7 low led → play the Q (top of the sequence)
You♠ K 10 9 7 low led → play the 10 (the 9 counts as the third honor)
Inserting the top of your touching cards can't cost, and it stops declarer finessing your middle cards cheaply.
Only two honors, not three Basic
Play low
You♠ Q J 8 3 low led → play the 3
With only two honors, splitting wastes one and helps declarer set up the suit.
A winner will vanish, or an entry must die Exception
Rise
Go up in second seat only for a concrete reason: (a) a sure winner is about to be ruffed or discarded — cash it now; (b) ducking gives declarer a free trick; or (c) rise (often the ace) to knock out dummy's only entry to a long suit.

You hold two honors above it Basic
Cover
Dummy♠ J 10 8 6You♠ A Q 2 jack led → play the ace

Dummy♠ 10 5 4You♠ K 7 2 10 led (no jack beside it) → cover with the king

Cover the last
Dummy♠ J 10 8You♠ K 2 jack led (10 sits beside it) → play low
Dummy♣ Q J 9You♣ K 6 5 queen led → duck; jack led next → cover
Don't cover the first of touching honors — the next one wins anyway. Cover the last, when it kills the finesse against partner's 10.

03When NOT to cover

Solid run in dummy (Q J 10 9) Exception
Don't cover
Dummy♠ Q J 10 9You♠ K 8 4 queen led → play low
Cover and you turn dummy's J-10-9 into three winners for them. Duck, and your king wins the third round.
Small doubleton honor under a strong dummy Exception
Don't cover
Dummy♦ A K 10 9You♦ J 4 10 led → play the 4
With A-K-10-9 over you, the jack promotes nothing — covering just gives it away.
Partner is known short in the suit Exception
Don't cover
When the bidding marks partner with a singleton or void — classically as declarer draws trumps — there's no partner's card to promote, so covering just gives up your own honor.

04Declarer (not dummy) leads the honor

Dummy has a card touching the lead Basic
Cover
Dummy♠ A J 10 6You♠ K 5 2 declarer leads the 9 → cover with the king
Dummy's 10 touches the 9, so the cover promotes — and stops the 9 running for a free finesse.
No touching card in dummy Basic
Don't cover
Dummy♠ A 8 6 4You♠ K 7 2 declarer leads the 10 → play low
No touching card means declarer holds the jack; covering would promote only his hand.

05Tricky positions

Double finesse — the cover promotes partner Exception
Cover the last
Dummy♣ Q J 3You♣ K 6 2Partner♣ 10 9 7 5 queen run → duck; jack led next → cover
Now Q-J-K-A are all gone and partner's 10 is high. Cover the first honor instead and dummy's jack finesses that 10 away.
Dummy is shorter than you Exception
Don't cover
Dummy♠ Q 6You♠ K 9 5 4 queen led → play low
A short dummy means declarer is long (say A J 10 x). Covering promotes only his cards, and can crash partner's honor.
Partner may hold a singleton honor Exception
Don't cover
Covering can drop both defensive honors on the same trick — “crashing.” If partner is marked short and you can't see a card to promote, duck.

Advanced — the surround play: with an interior holding like K‑10‑9 over dummy's jack, playing an intermediate (the 10) can trap dummy's honor. Situational — count the suit first.
